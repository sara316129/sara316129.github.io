5 Divers Died Instantly When Someone Opened the Wrong Hatch

📅 March 6, 2026 ⏱ 12 min read 💀 Deep Sea Horror

November 5, 1983. 4:00 AM. The North Sea.

The wind was howling. Waves crashed against the legs of the Byford Dolphin oil rig.

Inside the pressurized living chamber, five men had no idea they were about to die.

What happened next lasted less than half a second. But the horror would echo through the offshore diving industry for decades.

The World's Most Dangerous Job

Deep-sea saturation diving isn't just dangerous. It's a systematic defiance of human biology.

At 300 feet below the ocean surface, the pressure is so intense that every breath feels like breathing through molasses. Your body becomes a pressure vessel. Nitrogen dissolves into your blood, your tissues, your bones.

Come up too fast, and that nitrogen forms bubbles. Your blood fizzes like a shaken soda can. Divers call it "the bends." Doctors call it decompression sickness. Either way, it's excruciating and often fatal.

The Byford Dolphin operated in some of the harshest conditions on Earth - the North Sea in winter

The solution? Don't decompress at all. Stay pressurized for weeks.

This is saturation diving. You live in a metal tube barely bigger than a closet. The air is so thick it distorts your voice. The helium they mix in to prevent nitrogen narcosis makes you sound like Donald Duck.

You eat, sleep, and breathe at nine times normal atmospheric pressure. For 28 days straight.

Only at the end of the job do you decompress - slowly, over several days, in controlled conditions.

It's the only way to work at extreme depths for extended periods. And it's one of the most dangerous professions on the planet.

The Byford Dolphin

The Byford Dolphin wasn't just any rig. It was a semi-submersible drilling platform - essentially a floating factory for extracting oil from the seafloor.

Built in 1974, it operated in the Norwegian sector of the North Sea. The same waters that had claimed countless ships throughout history. Cold. Dark. Unforgiving.

By 1983, the rig had been operating for nearly a decade. Hundreds of divers had worked from its saturation diving system without major incident.

That spotless safety record was about to end.

The Men

Five men were involved in the incident that night. Each one a professional. Each one experienced.

Inside the Chamber:

Edwin Arthur Coward, 35, from the UK. Veteran diver. Married with two children.

Roy P. Lucas, 38, from the UK. Over a decade of diving experience.

Bjørn Giæver Bergersen, 29, from Norway. Considered one of the best in the business.

Truls Hellevik, 34, from Norway. Known for his meticulous attention to safety protocols.

Outside the Chamber:

William Crammond, 32, dive tender. His job was to assist the pressurized divers from the outside. He had done this hundreds of times.

These weren't rookies. These were men who understood the risks. Men who had spent years working in one of the most hostile environments imaginable.

None of that would matter.

How Saturation Diving Works

To understand what went wrong, you need to understand the system.

The Byford Dolphin had two main components: the living chamber and the diving bell.

The living chamber was a pressurized tube where divers lived for weeks at a time. Temperature controlled. Air filtered. Maintained at the same pressure as their working depth - in this case, nine atmospheres.

The diving bell was essentially an elevator. When it was time to work, divers would transfer from the living chamber into the bell. The bell would be lowered 300 feet to the seafloor. They'd do their work. Then the bell would bring them back up to the rig.

Crucially, the bell remained pressurized the entire time. When it docked with the living chamber, a sealed trunk connected them. The pressure had to equalize perfectly before anyone could move between the two.

Saturation diving equipment must maintain perfect pressure - any failure is catastrophic

The procedure for disconnecting the bell from the chamber was precise. Every step mattered. Every sequence was critical.

First, you had to ensure the pressure was equalized.

Then you had to seal the chamber.

Only then could you release the clamps holding the bell to the trunk.

Do it out of order, and... well, no one had ever done it out of order. Not on the Byford Dolphin. Not anywhere.

Until November 5, 1983.

The Mistake

4:00 AM. The dive team had just completed their shift.

Four divers were inside the living chamber: Coward, Lucas, Bergersen, and Hellevik. They were relaxing, probably looking forward to a hot meal and some sleep.

William Crammond was outside, working as the dive tender. His job was to disconnect the diving bell now that the divers were safely back in the chamber.

It's not clear exactly what went wrong. The investigation would later point to human error, but the exact sequence of events remains disputed.

What is clear: someone opened the clamp securing the bell to the chamber trunk before the chamber was properly sealed.

Inside the chamber: 9 atmospheres of pressure.

Outside: 1 atmosphere.

The laws of physics don't negotiate.

The Physics of Pressure

9 atmospheres means nine times the normal air pressure at sea level. That's roughly 132 pounds per square inch.

When that pressure is suddenly exposed to normal atmospheric pressure (14.7 psi), the pressure differential is massive - about 117 psi trying to equalize.

For reference, a car tire is usually inflated to 32 psi. Imagine more than three times that amount of pressure difference acting on everything inside the chamber. Including human bodies.

The equalization happened in 0.04 seconds.

Four hundredths of a second.

Faster than a human can blink.

What Happens to a Human Body

When pressure drops that suddenly, the nitrogen dissolved in your body doesn't have time to be exhaled. It forms bubbles. Everywhere.

In your blood. In your brain. In your spinal fluid. In every organ. In every tissue.

But at 9 atmospheres dropping to 1 atmosphere in 0.04 seconds, it's not just bubbles. It's catastrophic expansion.

Your blood doesn't just bubble. It boils.

Your organs don't just rupture. They explode.

And if you're near the opening where the pressure is escaping...

Truls Hellevik

Truls Hellevik was standing closest to the opening when the clamp released.

The pressure differential was so extreme that his body was forced through a 60-centimeter opening. That's 24 inches. About the size of a large dinner plate.

The human torso is significantly wider than 24 inches.

His body didn't fit through that opening. Not intact, anyway.

The investigation report describes his remains being found 10 meters (33 feet) away from the chamber. The medical examiner's report is not public, but witnesses at the scene described it as the most horrific thing they'd ever seen.

Some details are still classified out of respect for the family. What is known is that death was instantaneous. There was no suffering. His brain would have ceased functioning before pain signals could even register.

The Others

Edwin Coward, Roy Lucas, and Bjørn Bergersen were also inside the chamber.

They didn't get forced through the opening, but the result was just as fatal.

Every organ ruptured simultaneously. Every blood vessel burst. The nitrogen in their tissues expanded so violently that autopsies revealed massive internal trauma inconsistent with any known injury mechanism.

One report noted that the bodies appeared to have been "inflated from within" before the pressure released, causing catastrophic damage to all soft tissues.

All three died instantly. Like Hellevik, there was no time for pain. No time for fear. One moment they existed. The next, they didn't.

William Crammond

William Crammond was standing outside the chamber when the clamp released.

The blast of air escaping from the chamber at supersonic speed hit him like a bomb. The pressure wave alone was enough to cause massive trauma.

But it wasn't just air. It was debris. Pieces of equipment. And worse.

Crammond was thrown against a bulkhead. He died from blunt force trauma and multiple internal injuries.

Five men. All dead in less than half a second.

The North Sea has claimed countless lives - the Byford Dolphin disaster remains one of the worst industrial accidents in offshore history

The Investigation

The Norwegian Petroleum Directorate launched an immediate investigation.

The findings were clear: human error. Someone had opened the clamp out of sequence.

But why?

Several theories emerged:

Fatigue

It was 4:00 AM. The crew had been working for hours. Fatigue impairs judgment. It slows reaction times. It makes people skip steps they normally wouldn't.

Some investigators believed simple exhaustion led to the fatal mistake.

Inadequate Training

While the divers themselves were highly trained, the dive tenders and support crew sometimes received less rigorous instruction. The procedure for disconnecting the bell was complex. Maybe it wasn't properly understood.

Equipment Design

Others pointed to the system itself. Why was it physically possible to open the clamp before the chamber was sealed? Shouldn't there be an interlock? A fail-safe?

The equipment design allowed a fatal sequence of actions. That, some argued, was the real cause.

Communication Failure

With multiple crew members involved in the procedure, clear communication was essential. Radio chatter. Hand signals. Confirmations.

If someone assumed the chamber was sealed when it wasn't, a miscommunication could lead to disaster.

The investigation couldn't definitively determine which factor was primary. Likely it was a combination. Fatigue plus inadequate training plus equipment design plus communication failure.

A perfect storm of small failures culminating in catastrophe.

The Aftermath

The Byford Dolphin disaster sent shockwaves through the offshore drilling industry.

Within months, new regulations were implemented:

Mandatory Safety Interlocks: Chambers could no longer be opened while pressurized. Physical locks and redundant systems made the fatal sequence impossible.

Enhanced Training: All personnel involved in saturation diving operations had to undergo rigorous certification. No exceptions.

Communication Protocols: Standardized verbal confirmations became mandatory for every step of every procedure.

Crew Rest Requirements: Maximum shift lengths were reduced. Fatigue management became a priority.

Independent Verification: Critical procedures required two people to verify each step.

The changes worked. No similar accident has occurred since.

But the cost of those improvements was five lives.

The Rig Continues

The Byford Dolphin didn't shut down after the disaster.

It couldn't. Oil production in the North Sea was too important. Too lucrative.

The rig was repaired. New safety systems were installed. And within months, it was back in operation.

Divers had to live and work in the same chambers where their colleagues had died. They had to use the same systems, albeit with new safety features.

Some refused. They quit the profession entirely. The psychological trauma was too much.

Others stayed. They understood the risks. They trusted the new systems. And they needed the money. Saturation diving pays exceptionally well precisely because it's so dangerous.

The Byford Dolphin operated for another 26 years. It was finally decommissioned in 2009.

During those years, thousands of divers worked from the platform. Most never knew the full details of what happened in 1983. Those who did rarely spoke about it.

The Question That Remains

The official investigation concluded. The families received compensation. The regulations changed.

But one question has never been satisfactorily answered:

Why did an experienced crew member make such a catastrophic mistake?

Everyone involved was trained. Everyone knew the procedure. Everyone understood the consequences.

And yet, someone opened that clamp.

Was it truly just fatigue? Or was there something else? A distraction? A miscommunication more complex than the investigation revealed?

We'll never know for certain. The person responsible didn't survive to explain.

Lessons Learned

The Byford Dolphin disaster is now a case study in engineering courses, safety training programs, and risk management seminars worldwide.

It demonstrates several critical principles:

Fail-Safes Are Essential: If a fatal sequence of actions is possible, someone will eventually make that mistake. Equipment must be designed to prevent it.

Human Error Is Inevitable: No amount of training eliminates the possibility of mistakes. Systems must account for human fallibility.

Fatigue Kills: Exhausted workers make deadly mistakes. Rest requirements aren't optional.

Every Death Is Preventable: In hindsight, every accident has warning signs. The challenge is recognizing them before tragedy strikes.

Modern Saturation Diving

Today, saturation diving is safer than ever. But it remains one of the most dangerous professions in the world.

The men and women who do this work know the risks. They understand that they're living in an artificial environment where the margin for error is essentially zero.

One malfunctioning valve. One communication error. One moment of inattention.

That's all it takes.

But they also know that the work is essential. Offshore oil platforms. Underwater construction. Submarine rescue. Scientific research.

All of these require people willing to descend into the darkness and live under crushing pressure for weeks at a time.

And every one of them knows the story of the Byford Dolphin.

Every one of them has looked at the safety systems designed because five men died in 1983.

Every one of them has wondered: Could it happen again?

Remembering the Victims

It's easy to focus on the horror. The physics. The mechanics of what went wrong.

But five men died that morning. Five families lost fathers, husbands, sons.

Edwin Arthur Coward left behind a wife and two children who never saw him again.

Roy P. Lucas had been planning to retire after this job. He never made it home.

Bjørn Giæver Bergersen was 29 years old. His whole life ahead of him.

Truls Hellevik was known as meticulous. Safety-conscious. It didn't save him.

William Crammond was just doing his job. Helping others. He paid with his life.

They deserve to be remembered not as statistics. Not as cautionary tales. But as people.

People who went to work one morning and never came home.

💭 Final Thought

"In the deep ocean, nature does not forgive. The Byford Dolphin taught us that the price of complacency is paid in lives. Those five men didn't die in vain - they died so that others wouldn't have to." - Norwegian Petroleum Safety Authority, 2003
