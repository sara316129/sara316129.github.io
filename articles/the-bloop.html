An Underwater Sound Louder Than Any Animal on Earth

📅 February 28, 2026⏱ 6 min read🌊 Deep Sea

August 19, 1997. NOAA Hydrophone Array. The Pacific Ocean.

Scientists at the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration were reviewing recordings from their underwater microphone network - a Cold War relic originally designed to detect Soviet submarines.

What they found on tape that day had no explanation. A sound so loud it had been detected across 3,000 miles of open ocean. Louder than any known animal on Earth. Louder than a blue whale - the loudest creature ever recorded - by a factor of several times over.

They called it "The Bloop."

The Listening Network Nobody Talks About

SOSUS - the Sound Surveillance System - is a network of hydrophones (underwater microphones) installed on the ocean floor by the US Navy during the Cold War. Its original mission was to track Soviet submarines by listening for their engine sounds.

When the Cold War ended, much of SOSUS was declassified and handed to NOAA scientists, who began using it to listen to the ocean itself. Whale songs. Earthquakes. Underwater volcanoes. The deep ocean turned out to be extraordinarily loud.

But nothing had prepared them for what 1997 brought.

The deep Pacific Ocean - where a sound louder than any known creature was recorded in 1997

The Sound Itself

The Bloop was detected by hydrophones 3,000 miles apart simultaneously - meaning its source radiated enough energy to travel across the width of an entire ocean basin. Its frequency was extremely low, meaning it came from something either very large or very powerful.

Its acoustic profile most resembled a living creature. The sound rose in pitch rapidly over about a minute, suggesting something biological rather than geological. It matched no known species.

The location of origin was triangulated to a point in the South Pacific Ocean - roughly 1,500 miles off the southern tip of South America. Interestingly, this region sits near the coordinates H.P. Lovecraft chose for his fictional sunken city of R'lyeh in 1926 - a coincidence that delighted internet users for years.

🌊 The Numbers

Detection range: approximately 3,000 miles (5,000 km) - captured simultaneously by multiple hydrophone arrays. Volume: significantly louder than the loudest confirmed animal sound ever recorded. Duration: approximately 1 minute. Frequency: infrasound range, too low for human hearing without slowing.

What Scientists Actually Think

NOAA's official explanation, confirmed by follow-up research: icequake. A massive chunk of ice - potentially an iceberg the size of a small country - breaking free from an Antarctic glacier.

Icequakes are known to produce extremely loud infrasound with a rising pitch profile. The 1997 source location is consistent with Antarctic iceberg activity. When scientists compared The Bloop to confirmed icequake recordings made after better instrumentation was deployed, the similarities were compelling.

The explanation is almost certainly correct. Almost certainly.

Why People Still Wonder

The problem with the icequake explanation is what it requires: an icequake of absolutely unprecedented scale. No icequake before or since has produced anything close to The Bloop's amplitude. The noise was detected across 3,000 miles - no icequake has matched that reach.

Scientists point out that our understanding of icequake acoustics was limited in 1997. Modern recordings have captured large icequakes that are loud and unusual. The argument is that The Bloop was simply an extremely large version of a known phenomenon.

But the ocean covers 71% of Earth's surface. The average depth is 12,100 feet. Most of it has never been explored. The largest creatures ever found - giant squid, colossal squid - were mythological sea monsters until the 20th century.

Something is down there. We know almost nothing about it.

The deep ocean is Earth's final unexplored frontier - 95% of it has never been seen by human eyes

💭 Final Thought

NOAA says it was an icequake. That's probably true. But the ocean has given us the giant squid, the oarfish, the anglerfish, creatures that looked like monsters until we finally caught one. The Bloop came from a place we've never fully explored. And it sounded, according to the scientists who studied it, like something alive.
