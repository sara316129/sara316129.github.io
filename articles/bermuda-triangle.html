Over 100 Ships and Planes Vanished in the Bermuda Triangle

📅 February 23, 2026⏱ 12 min read🔺 Paranormal

50 ships. 20 planes. Thousands of lives. A roughly 500,000 square mile stretch of the Atlantic Ocean between Miami, Bermuda, and Puerto Rico.

The Bermuda Triangle. The Devil's Triangle. The Graveyard of the Atlantic.

For decades, it has been the world's most famous geographical mystery - an area where ships and aircraft vanish without explanation, without wreckage, without any trace of the hundreds of people aboard them.

But what is actually happening there?

The Story That Started Everything

The Bermuda Triangle as a cultural phenomenon was created almost entirely by one article. In 1964, writer Vincent Gaddis published a piece in Argosy magazine titled "The Deadly Bermuda Triangle" - coining the name and compiling a list of disappearances he claimed were linked by geography and supernatural cause.

The concept exploded. Charles Berlitz published a bestselling book in 1974. Films followed. The Triangle became the world's most famous ocean.

What Gaddis and Berlitz didn't disclose was that many of the disappearances they cited had mundane explanations that had been solved years earlier, or that their geographic coordinates placed the incidents well outside the Triangle's boundaries.

The Cases That Are Real

Strip away the mythology, and a core of genuinely unexplained disappearances remains.

USS Cyclops, 1918: 306 people. 542 feet of steel. No distress signal. No wreckage ever found. America's greatest naval mystery. The Triangle's most undeniable case.

Flight 19, 1945: Five Navy aircraft, 14 men. The navigator became confused about his position in the exact region. Flew the wrong direction. Disappeared. A search plane sent after them also vanished.

Star Tiger, 1948: A British South American Airways Tudor IV aircraft vanished between the Azores and Bermuda with 31 people aboard. No mayday. No wreckage. Weather was poor but not extreme. Investigation conclusion: "no more can be said than that they have disappeared."

Star Ariel, 1949: Another Tudor IV. Same company. Bermuda to Nassau. Sent one brief routine message that it was switching to a new radio frequency. Never heard from again. 20 people. No trace.

The Real Numbers

Lloyd's of London - which insures most major vessels - does not charge higher premiums for ships crossing the Bermuda Triangle. The US Coast Guard does not recognize it as an unusually dangerous area. NOAA states officially: "the Triangle does not exist as an official geographical area." Yet the disappearances are real.

What Science Actually Says

The Triangle is one of only two places on Earth where true north and magnetic north align - meaning compass variations that affect other regions don't apply here. This eliminates one proposed explanation.

The Gulf Stream - one of Earth's most powerful ocean currents - flows directly through the Triangle at up to 5 mph. Wreckage from a sinking ship would be dispersed hundreds of miles within days. No recovery of surface debris doesn't mean no sinking - it means the Gulf Stream carried everything away.

Methane hydrates - bubbles of gas trapped in undersea sediment - have been proposed as an explanation. A large methane eruption could theoretically reduce water density enough to sink a ship without warning. This hypothesis has not been proven experimentally, but the Triangle does sit over methane rich geology.

The weather in the region is notoriously unpredictable. Sudden waterspouts. Violent squalls that develop faster than weather systems elsewhere. Fog banks that appear and vanish in minutes.

The Insurance Company's Opinion

The most compelling piece of evidence against the Triangle's supernatural reputation comes from actuaries.

Lloyd's of London has been insuring ships since 1686. If the Bermuda Triangle were genuinely more dangerous than other ocean regions, their actuarial data - based on centuries of maritime records - would reflect it. Ships crossing the Triangle would cost more to insure.

They don't. The rates are standard.

Insurance companies have no interest in mythology. They care about statistical risk. By the measure of people whose livelihood depends on accurate risk assessment, the Bermuda Triangle is not unusually dangerous.

Then Why Do Things Vanish?

The honest answer: because the ocean is dangerous, enormous, and indifferent to human presence.

The Triangle is one of the most heavily trafficked ocean regions on Earth - thousands of ships and aircraft cross it every year. Statistically, some percentage will encounter problems. The ones that sink in deep water leave no trace. The Gulf Stream ensures any surface debris is gone within days.

The Triangle's apparent mystery, argued sceptic researcher Lawrence Kusche in his 1975 book "The Bermuda Triangle Mystery - Solved," exists because the original investigators didn't do their homework. Berlitz and Gaddis included storms that were well documented, accidents whose causes were known, ships that disappeared far outside the Triangle's boundaries. The mystery, Kusche argued, was largely manufactured.

But the USS Cyclops, the Star Tiger, and the Star Ariel remain genuinely unexplained. Real ships. Real people. Real silence.

💭 Final Thought

The Bermuda Triangle probably isn't supernatural. But it isn't nothing, either. Strip away the mythology and what remains is real: ships and aircraft that disappeared without trace, in relatively calm conditions, with no wreckage ever found. The ocean doesn't always give back what it takes. The Triangle just takes more than most places notice.
