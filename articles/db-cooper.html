He Hijacked a Plane, Got $200,000 and Jumped Out

📅 February 24, 2026⏱ 10 min read🪂 True Crime

November 24, 1971. Portland International Airport, Oregon.

A man in a dark suit bought a one way ticket on Northwest Orient Flight 305 to Seattle. He paid cash. He gave his name as Dan Cooper. He ordered a bourbon and soda, lit a cigarette, and waited.

Forty-five minutes later, 10,000 feet over the Pacific Northwest wilderness, he jumped out of the back of a commercial airliner with $200,000 in cash strapped to his body.

He was never found. He remains the only unsolved case of air piracy in American aviation history.

The Hijacking

After takeoff, the man in seat 18C passed a note to flight attendant Florence Schaffner. She assumed it was a phone number and tucked it in her pocket unread. He leaned over and said: "Miss, you'd better look at that note. I have a bomb."

The note described a bomb in his briefcase. He wanted $200,000 in negotiable twenty dollar bills, four parachutes - two front, two back - and a fuel truck waiting in Seattle.

He was calm throughout. He ordered another bourbon. He allowed the other 36 passengers to deplane in Seattle while negotiations continued. He was polite. Professional. The flight attendants later described him as "thoughtful" - he offered to order food for the crew from the catering truck.

Flight 305 climbed back into the night sky after refueling in Seattle - somewhere over Washington State, the man in seat 18C vanished

The Jump

The FBI delivered the money - $200,000 in marked $20 bills, serial numbers recorded - and four parachutes. The plane refueled. Cooper directed the crew to fly to Mexico City at low altitude and minimum speed, with the landing gear down and flaps at 15 degrees.

At 8:11 PM, somewhere over the Cascade Mountains of Washington or Oregon, the crew felt the rear airstair drop. A pressure change. Then nothing. Cooper had jumped. In the dark. In a rainstorm. In a business suit. At 10,000 feet. Into the wilderness.

The FBI code named the investigation "NORJAK" - Northwest Hijacking. It became the Bureau's longest-running active investigation in history. They pursued it for 45 years before officially suspending active investigation in 2016.

🪂 The Evidence

$5,800 in marked DB Cooper bills found by an 8 year old boy along the Columbia River in 1980 - partially matching serial numbers. One tie and two of the four parachutes were left on the plane. No body. No parachute. No remaining money. No confirmed identity after 54 years of investigation.

Did He Survive?

This is the central question — and most experts who have analyzed the case believe the answer is no.

Cooper jumped at night, in a rainstorm, over heavily forested mountains, in a business suit with no cold weather gear. The temperature outside the aircraft was estimated at -7°C. He had no experience apparent in his behavior that would suggest military parachute training for wilderness survival. The parachutes he chose were not the best available - he took a dummy training chute and an old military surplus chute.

Former FBI lead investigator Ralph Himmelsbach spent his career convinced Cooper died in the jump and his remains were swallowed by the dense Oregon wilderness.

But the $5,800 found in 1980 complicates this. The bills were found in a location broadly consistent with the probable jump zone but along a riverbank - suggesting they floated there. Some analysts believe Cooper survived and spent the money, losing a small bundle in the Columbia River system. Others think the bundle arrived there from a crash site upstream.

The 800 Suspects

Over five decades, the FBI investigated more than 800 named suspects. All were cleared. The Bureau has received tips from people claiming to be Cooper himself, from relatives of deceased men who confessed on their deathbeds, and from amateur investigators with elaborate theories.

The most famous deathbed confession came in 2011, when a woman named Marla Cooper said her uncle, Lynn Doyle Cooper, told her he was D.B. Cooper before he died. The FBI tested his DNA and could not confirm a match.

In 2016, the FBI announced it was suspending active investigation - but the case technically remains open. The $200,000 in bills, if found, would still be identified by their serial numbers.

The Cascade Mountains of Washington state - dense, dark, unforgiving wilderness into which D.B. Cooper jumped and never emerged

The Legacy

D.B. Cooper became an American folk hero almost immediately. He appeared on t-shirts and bumper stickers within weeks of the hijacking. Books, documentaries, songs. The town of Ariel, Washington - near the probable jump zone - has held an annual "D.B. Cooper Days" festival.

His hijacking directly caused the metal detector security systems now used in every airport worldwide. Before November 1971, passengers boarded commercial aircraft without any security screening whatsoever. Cooper's jump changed that forever.

The real man - whoever he was - either died alone in cold wilderness that November night, or lived out his life somewhere, watching the world make him a legend, and never said a word.

💭 Final Thought

D.B. Cooper jumped into a rainstorm over a mountain wilderness in a business suit with $200,000. He was either brilliant or suicidal, and we still don't know which. Fifty four years later, airport security, FBI cold case files, and an annual festival in Washington state are the only confirmed things he left behind.
