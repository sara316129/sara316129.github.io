This 600 Year Old Book Can't Be Read By Anyone

📅 February 21, 2026⏱ 9 min read📖 Historical

1912. A crumbling villa outside Rome.

Rare book dealer Wilfrid Voynich was browsing through a collection of old manuscripts when he found something extraordinary. A small book, written on vellum, covered in text he had never seen before. An unknown script. Illustrations of plants that matched no species in any botanical catalog. Astronomical diagrams. Strange figures bathing in green liquid. Page after page of meticulous, confident writing in an alphabet that no scholar on Earth could read.

The book was at least 600 years old. It had been written by someone who clearly understood what they were writing. And nobody - in over a century of trying - has ever deciphered a single sentence of it.

What the Book Looks Like

The Voynich Manuscript is a 240-page book - many pages have been lost; it may have originally been longer. Every page is covered in a flowing, left-to-right text written in an alphabet of approximately 20-25 characters. No single character matches any known writing system.

The illustrations fall into recognizable sections. Botanical pages show plants - but most match no known species. Astronomical pages show zodiac symbols, stars, and circular diagrams. A "biological" section shows small nude figures bathing in pools connected by elaborate pipe-work. A cosmological section shows interlocking discs and text. A pharmaceutical section shows containers and plant parts.

The illustrations appear to be functional - drawn to accompany specific text. Whatever the writer was communicating, they were explaining something systematic and detailed.

The Voynich Manuscript's script is written fluently and confidently - whoever wrote it knew exactly what they were saying

What We Know For Certain

Radiocarbon dating in 2009 established the vellum (calfskin parchment) was made between 1404 and 1438. This is one of the few hard facts about the manuscript.

The ink is carbon-based and consistent with the vellum's age. The book was not created recently and antedated to deceive - the carbon dating confirms the vellum itself is medieval.

Statistical analysis of the text shows it follows what linguists call Zipf's Law - a mathematical pattern found in all natural human languages. Random nonsense doesn't follow this pattern. The text has the statistical fingerprint of a real language.

The manuscript has a known provenance trail from the early 1600s - it appears in correspondence from 1639 referencing Emperor Rudolf II of Germany (who ruled 1576-1612) supposedly paying 600 gold ducats for it.

📖 The Facts

Age: vellum dated 1404-1438 by radiocarbon analysis. Pages: 240 remaining (possibly more lost). Script: approximately 20-25 unique characters, left-to-right writing direction. Statistical profile: matches natural language patterns. Identified words: zero. Deciphered sentences: zero.

500 Years of Failure

The list of people who have tried and failed to crack the Voynich Manuscript reads like a hall of fame of human intellectual achievement.

During World War II, America's top military codebreakers - the people who cracked enemy naval ciphers that changed the course of the war - spent years on the Voynich Manuscript in their spare time. They made no progress.

Elizabethan occultist and royal advisor John Dee apparently tried to decode an unknown manuscript in the 1580s that many historians believe was the Voynich. He failed.

In 2004, computer scientist Gordon Rugg proposed the text was a hoax created with a Cardan grille - a grid device that generates pseudo-random text. His theory generated significant attention and then significant counter-evidence. Linguistic analysis showed patterns inconsistent with a Cardan grille hoax.

In 2019, Bristol academic Gerard Cheshire published a paper claiming the manuscript was written in "proto-Romance" - an ancestor of modern Romance languages. Academic linguists reviewed his work and comprehensively rejected it. The journal later issued an expression of concern about the paper.

The Leading Theories

An unknown natural language: The manuscript is written in a real language that became extinct without leaving other records. This is linguistically possible, though unusual for a seemingly well-educated European author.

A constructed language: A deliberate invented language, created for private use or as an intellectual exercise. Several medieval scholars created private scripts. The 15th century had a fashion for such things.

An elaborate code: The text encodes Latin or another known language using a substitution cipher complex enough to resist five centuries of attack. This would require a level of cryptographic sophistication not seen in Europe until the 20th century.

A hoax: Created to extract money from Rudolf II, who paid handsomely for curiosities. But the statistical language properties are hard to fake convincingly - generating text that follows Zipf's Law deliberately requires modern computing power.

The Voynich Manuscript sits at Yale University's Beinecke Rare Book Library - available for anyone to study, understood by nobody

Where It Sits Today

The Voynich Manuscript is held at Yale University's Beinecke Rare Book and Manuscript Library, catalogued as MS 408. Yale has digitized the entire manuscript - every page is available online at full resolution, free to anyone.

The entire human intellectual establishment - every linguist, cryptographer, computer scientist, and historian who wants to try - has access to the complete text. AI systems have been applied. Machine learning models trained on thousands of languages have found no match.

Whatever the Voynich Manuscript says, it has kept its secret for over 600 years. It may keep it forever.

💭 Final Thought

Six hundred years ago, someone sat down with a pen and carefully wrote 240 pages of something they clearly understood completely. Military codebreakers, AI systems, linguists, and cryptographers have all tried. All failed. The book sits open at Yale, every page visible to anyone. Still saying nothing. Still saying everything. Still completely, maddeningly silent.
