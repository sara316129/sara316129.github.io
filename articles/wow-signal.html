We Received a Signal From Space in 1977

📅 February 22, 2026⏱ 7 min read📡 Space Mystery

August 15, 1977. The Big Ear Radio Observatory, Ohio.

Astronomer Jerry Ehman was reviewing data printouts from the previous night's sky scan. Numbers and letters scrolled past - the steady background hiss of the universe, the noise of deep space. Then he reached a sequence that made him grab his pen.

Six characters: 6EQUJ5. A signal so strong, so perfectly structured, so startlingly different from everything around it that Ehman circled it and wrote in the margin - in red ballpoint - a single word:

Wow!

That word gave the most tantalizing possible signal in the history of SETI its name. The Wow! Signal has never been explained. And it has never been heard again.

What the Signal Was

The Big Ear telescope was part of the Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence - SETI. Its job was to scan the sky for radio signals that might differ from natural background noise in ways that could indicate an artificial, intelligent origin.

The Wow! Signal arrived on a frequency of 1420.4056 MHz - the precise frequency of hydrogen, the most common element in the universe. Scientists had long theorized that any civilization attempting to broadcast across interstellar distances would logically choose this frequency, as any spacefaring species would know hydrogen's signature.

The signal lasted 72 seconds - exactly the time it takes for Big Ear to scan a fixed point in the sky as the Earth rotates. This means the signal wasn't terrestrial interference. It came from a fixed point in space.

The Big Ear radio telescope detected a 72 second signal from space — then never heard it again despite decades of searching

6EQUJ5 - What It Means

The printout used numbers and letters to represent signal strength. 0-9 meant weak signals. Letters A onward represented increasing strength above that scale. The sequence 6EQUJ5 represents the signal rising from nothing, peaking at intensity "U" - more than 30 times the background noise - then falling away again.

The profile perfectly matched what scientists had theorized an extraterrestrial narrowband signal would look like as the Earth rotated past its source. It matched the predicted signal curve for a point source in deep space better than any signal SETI had ever received before or since.

📡 The Signal's Profile

Frequency: 1420.4056 MHz (hydrogen line - the predicted alien broadcast frequency). Duration: 72 seconds (Earth's rotation period past a fixed sky point). Intensity: 30x background noise at peak. Origin: fixed point in space in the direction of constellation Sagittarius. Source: unknown. Repeated transmissions: never detected.

The Search for a Repeat

Jerry Ehman and the Big Ear team turned the telescope back to the same coordinates immediately. Nothing. They monitored the region for months. Silence. Other observatories around the world pointed their dishes at the same coordinates. Nothing.

In 1987 and 1989, the META survey - a dedicated SETI program - specifically targeted the Wow! Signal coordinates. Nothing. In 1995 and 1996, the Ohio State program tried again. Nothing. In 2020, a renewed search with modern equipment - far more sensitive than anything available in 1977 - found nothing at those coordinates.

Whatever produced the Wow! Signal transmitted once, for 72 seconds, and then stopped.

What Could It Have Been?

Several explanations have been proposed - and all of them are unsatisfying.

Interstellar scintillation: Radio waves from distant sources can be amplified by gas clouds between stars, temporarily boosting a weak signal to detectable strength. This could explain a one-time detection - but the frequency alignment with hydrogen is suspicious coincidence.

A passing comet: In 2017, astronomer Antonio Paris proposed that a hydrogen emitting comet passing through the signal's source region in 1977 could have caused the signal. Skeptics pointed out the proposed comets weren't charted in that location at that time, and the frequency didn't match comet hydrogen emission profiles well enough.

Satellite reflection or military signal: Classified military satellites operating on unusual frequencies could theoretically explain it. The US government has never confirmed or denied this.

Extraterrestrial intelligence: A single, deliberate broadcast from a technologically advanced civilization, aimed toward our sun, received once and never again - perhaps because it was a beacon, a pulse, not a continuous signal.

Sagittarius - the constellation from which the Wow! Signal appeared to originate, approximately 120-200 light years away

Jerry Ehman's Own View

Ehman himself has been cautious over the decades. He has said the signal is "consistent with" an extraterrestrial origin but refuses to claim it definitively was one. He has called the comet explanation "far-fetched." He has said he wished it had been received again.

He has also said: "I am still waiting for someone to tell me what it was."

Nobody has.

💭 Final Thought

"I am still waiting for someone to tell me what it was." - Jerry Ehman, 2010. For 72 seconds in August 1977, something in the direction of Sagittarius sent a signal that matched every theoretical prediction for what extraterrestrial intelligence would sound like. Then it never came again. We are still listening.
