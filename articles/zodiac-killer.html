The Zodiac Killer Sent Secret Codes and Disappeared Forever

📅 February 26, 2026⏱ 11 min read🔪 True Crime

December 20, 1968. 11:15 PM. Benicia, California.

Two teenagers - David Faraday and Betty Lou Jensen - parked on a lovers' lane on Lake Herman Road. They were on their first date.

Someone walked up to the car and shot them both. David was killed instantly. Betty Lou ran. She was shot five times in the back before she reached the road.

Nobody saw anything. No motive. No suspect. No DNA databases. No surveillance cameras. Just two teenagers dead on a dark road, and a killer who had just started.

A Killer Who Needed an Audience

What separated the Zodiac from other serial killers was ego. He didn't just want to kill. He wanted to be famous for it.

On July 31, 1969 - after attacking two more couples and killing three people - the Zodiac sent simultaneous letters to three Bay Area newspapers. Each letter included one third of a cipher he claimed would reveal his identity.

He demanded the newspapers publish the ciphers on their front pages, or he would "go on a kill rampage" over the weekend. All three newspapers published.

He was just beginning.

The Zodiac sent taunting letters to newspapers for years - including encoded ciphers he claimed would reveal his identity

The Ciphers

The Zodiac sent at least four ciphers to police and newspapers. Only one has been definitively solved. Two remain unbroken to this day.

The first cipher - the 408 character message split across three newspapers - was decoded within a week by a Salinas schoolteacher named Donald Harden and his wife Bettye. The message began: "I like killing people because it is so much fun." It promised the killer would be reborn as a slave in the afterlife from all the people he killed. It did not reveal his name, as promised.

The 340 character cipher sat unsolved for 51 years - through the entire careers of professional cryptographers and the invention of modern computing — until December 2020, when a team of amateur codebreakers in Australia, Belgium, and the United States cracked it. It was a boast. More threats. Still no name.

🔪 Confirmed Victims

David Faraday (17) and Betty Lou Jensen (16): December 20, 1968. Darlene Ferrin (22) and Michael Mageau (19, survived): July 4, 1969. Bryan Hartnell (20, survived) and Cecelia Shepard (22): September 27, 1969. Paul Stine (29), taxi driver: October 11, 1969. The Zodiac claimed up to 37 victims. Police confirmed five deaths. At least two more survived.

The Letter That Should Have Ended It

On October 13, 1969, the Zodiac called into the SFPD and boasted about killing taxi driver Paul Stine. He had left a piece of Stine's bloody shirt in his previous letter as proof.

Three officers had stopped a man matching his description near the scene minutes after the killing - but a radio dispatcher had incorrectly described the suspect as Black. The officers, looking for a Black suspect, let a white man walk away.

The Zodiac was almost certainly within arms' reach of capture. It didn't happen.

He wrote to newspapers mocking the police: "How can I remain anonymous, if you keep describing me to a T?"

Who Was He?

The Zodiac was never officially identified. Hundreds of suspects have been proposed over 57 years. San Francisco PD lists the case as "open."

The most prominent modern suspect is Arthur Leigh Allen - a convicted sex offender from Vallejo. He was investigated three times, polygraphed, had his property searched. The evidence was circumstantial but compelling: he wore a Zodiac watch, used the phrases the Zodiac used, and was identified by a survivor. His DNA didn't match the saliva on the Zodiac's letter stamps. He died in 1992, never charged.

In 2021, an independent research group calling themselves "The Case Breakers" claimed to have identified another suspect — Gary Francis Poste, a former Air Force veteran. The FBI reviewed their evidence and declined to comment.

The Zodiac Killer is almost certainly dead. He was likely active in 1968–1974. His identity went with him.

The Zodiac's coded messages tormented investigators for decades - some remain unsolved to this day

The Letter That Stopped

The Zodiac's last confirmed communication was a letter in 1974. After that - silence. No new letters. No new verified attacks. Just absence.

Did he die? Move? Stop? Nobody knows. The sudden silence of a killer who craved attention is almost as strange as the killings themselves.

Whatever happened, he achieved what he wanted. Fifty seven years later, his name is still known. His ciphers still discussed. His victims, unfortunately, often reduced to footnotes in a story about their killer.

💭 Final Thought

The Zodiac Killer murdered at least five people, terrorized an entire region, and mocked law enforcement for years. He was never caught, never identified, and almost certainly died quietly of old age while his victims' families grew old waiting for answers. The only cipher that truly mattered - his name - was never cracked.
