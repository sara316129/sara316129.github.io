5 Planes Vanished in 1945 - Why Won't the Navy Talk?

📅 February 27, 2026⏱ 9 min read✈️ Aviation Horror

December 5, 1945. 2:10 PM. Naval Air Station Fort Lauderdale, Florida.

Five TBF Avenger torpedo bombers taxied down the runway and lifted into a clear Florida sky. Fourteen experienced pilots and aircrew aboard. A routine navigation training exercise over the Bahamas. They would be back by dinner.

None of them ever came back.

And neither did the 13-man search plane sent to find them.

The Mission

Flight 19 was led by Lieutenant Charles Carroll Taylor, an experienced pilot with 2,509 flight hours. His four student pilots each had at least 300 hours. The mission: fly east over the Bahamas, bomb practice targets near Bimini, then continue on a triangular route north and southwest back to base. Simple. Clear skies. Calm seas.

At 3:45 PM - ninety minutes after takeoff - Lieutenant Robert Cox, flying a different mission, picked up a strange radio transmission.

"Calling tower. This is an emergency. We seem to be off course. We cannot see land."

Cox asked who was transmitting. The reply: "We are not sure of our position. We can't be sure of just where we are."

Five TBF Avengers flew east over the Atlantic on December 5, 1945, and never returned - their last transmissions describing confusion over location

The Strange Transmissions

Over the next three hours, radio operators at Fort Lauderdale monitored increasingly distressed communications from Flight 19. The transmissions were fragmented - radio equipment of the era was unreliable - but what came through was haunting.

Taylor apparently believed his compass had malfunctioned. He thought they were over the Florida Keys, south of their position. His solution was to fly north and east - which, if correct, would bring them to the Florida coast. But if they were actually over the Bahamas as planned, flying northeast would take them deeper into open ocean.

One of his student pilots suggested flying west - toward shore, any shore. Taylor overruled him. "We don't know if we're over the Atlantic or the Gulf of Mexico," he said.

They flew northeast. They flew into darkness. The transmissions became too weak to decode. Then they stopped.

📻 Taylor's Last Words

"All planes close up tight... we'll have to ditch unless landfall... when the first plane drops below ten gallons we all go down together." These were among the last coherent transmissions received from Flight 19. Taylor believed they were over the Gulf of Mexico. They were almost certainly over the Atlantic, flying away from shore.

The Search Plane That Also Vanished

At 7:27 PM, a Martin PBM Mariner flying boat with 13 crew took off from Naval Air Station Banana River to search for Flight 19. Twenty-three minutes later, a merchant vessel in the area reported seeing an explosion in the sky, followed by a burning oil slick on the water.

The Mariner was never found. No remains. No life preservers. No identifiable wreckage.

The Navy later determined the Mariner likely exploded mid-flight - the aircraft had a known fuel vapor problem and was nicknamed "the flying gas tank." A spark in the bilge could ignite accumulated vapors. This was a known risk, accepted as probable cause.

But the coincidence - the search plane lost while looking for the planes that were lost - became the foundation of the Bermuda Triangle legend.

The Investigation's Conclusion

The official Navy investigation concluded: "causes or reasons unknown." This honest admission of ignorance was seized upon as evidence of something sinister. In reality, it reflected the genuine uncertainty of investigating an accident over open ocean in 1945.

The most likely explanation: Lieutenant Taylor became confused about his position and flew the wrong direction. Running low on fuel over open ocean at night, the five aircraft likely ditched in rough seas - the weather had worsened significantly by evening. TBF Avengers were notoriously difficult to ditch; they tended to sink in seconds. The crews likely survived ditching but couldn't escape the rapidly sinking aircraft.

Heavy seas would have dispersed wreckage quickly. The ocean floor in that region is extremely deep. No trace has ever been found.

The Atlantic off the Bahamas - where 27 men and six aircraft vanished in the ocean's most famous mystery

The 27 Men

It's easy to get lost in the legend and forget the humans. Fourteen men on Flight 19. Thirteen men on the PBM Mariner. Twenty-seven people who didn't come home to their families in December 1945.

Lieutenant Taylor was posthumously exonerated after initial findings blamed his "failure of leadership." Subsequent investigation concluded he was experiencing equipment failure and genuine navigational confusion - not incompetence. He died not knowing where he was, trying to save men who trusted him.

💭 Final Thought

The most supernatural thing about Flight 19 is that it needs no supernatural explanation. A compass failure. A wrong turn. Darkness. Ocean. The Bermuda Triangle didn't swallow 27 men - a series of human errors in bad conditions did. The mystery isn't what took them. The mystery is why we needed it to be something more.
