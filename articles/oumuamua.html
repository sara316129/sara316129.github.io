An Object From Another Solar System Visited Us

📅 March 1, 2026⏱ 8 min read☄️ First Contact?

October 19, 2017. Haleakalā Observatory, Hawaii.

Astronomer Robert Weryk was scanning routine telescope data when he noticed something impossible. An object streaking through our solar system - moving far too fast to have originated here.

It had come from somewhere else. Another star. Another system. And it was accelerating away from the Sun in a way that violated every known law of physics for a simple rock.

Scientists named it 'Oumuamua - Hawaiian for "messenger from afar, arriving first." What it actually was remains the most hotly debated question in modern astronomy.

The First Visitor From Another Star

'Oumuamua was the first interstellar object ever detected passing through our solar system. Before 2017, astronomers had theorized such objects might exist. Nobody had ever seen one.

It had entered our solar system from the direction of the constellation Lyra, traveling at 58,900 mph. It swung around the Sun on September 9, 2017, getting to within 38 million kilometers of it - closer than Mercury's orbit. By the time telescopes found it, it was already leaving.

Scientists had weeks - maybe months - to study it before it disappeared forever.

'Oumuamua came from interstellar space - the first confirmed visitor from another solar system ever detected by humanity

It Didn't Look Like a Comet. Or an Asteroid.

As telescopes tracked 'Oumuamua, the data became stranger by the day.

Its shape was unlike anything in our solar system. The light curve - the pattern of reflected sunlight - showed it was either extremely elongated (like a cigar, perhaps 10 times longer than it was wide) or extremely flat (like a pancake or a disc). Normal solar system objects are roughly spherical.

Its color was dark red - consistent with organic material baked by billions of years of cosmic radiation. That part made sense.

What didn't make sense was what happened when it left.

🔭 The Object's Properties

Length: estimated 400-800 meters. Speed: 58,900 mph entering, accelerating to 87,000+ mph leaving. Shape: either cigar-like (10:1 ratio) or disc-like. Color: dark reddish. Origin: direction of constellation Lyra, near the star Vega. No detected outgassing. No coma. No tail. Nothing that looks like a comet.

The Acceleration That Broke the Rules

Here is the problem. When a comet passes near the Sun, solar heat vaporizes ice on its surface. This outgassing acts like a tiny rocket, pushing the comet and changing its trajectory slightly. Scientists call this non-gravitational acceleration.

'Oumuamua showed non-gravitational acceleration - it sped up in a way that pure gravity couldn't explain. But there was no outgassing detected. No vaporizing ice. No comet tail. No coma of gas around it. Nothing.

It accelerated away from the Sun like something was pushing it. And there was nothing visible doing the pushing.

Every proposed explanation required something unusual. Astronomers debated hydrogen ice, neon ice, tidal fragmentation, exotic dust clouds. None of the explanations were satisfying. All of them required properties or behaviors never before observed in any object, anywhere.

The Harvard Professor Who Said "Alien"

In November 2018, Harvard astronomy professor Avi Loeb and postdoctoral researcher Shmuel Bialy published a paper in The Astrophysical Journal suggesting that 'Oumuamua's acceleration was consistent with a light sail - a spacecraft propelled by solar radiation pressure.

To work, a light sail needs to be extremely thin and flat - like a pancake of material just a fraction of a millimeter thick. This matched 'Oumuamua's unusual shape data.

Loeb did not say definitively that 'Oumuamua was an alien spacecraft. He said it was a hypothesis worth investigating seriously. The scientific community reacted with furious disagreement. Colleagues publicly called his paper irresponsible. Loeb said the reaction proved scientists were too afraid of the extraordinary answer to consider it fairly.

By the time humanity noticed 'Oumuamua, it was already leaving - and it accelerated as it went, in ways physics can't explain

Gone Forever

'Oumuamua is now approximately 4.6 billion miles from Earth, heading out of the solar system permanently. No spacecraft can catch it. No telescope can track it anymore. Whatever it was, it's gone.

In 2019, a second interstellar object - Borisov - was detected. It looked completely ordinary: a regular comet with outgassing, a tail, a spherical shape. Everything 'Oumuamua wasn't.

The contrast made 'Oumuamua stranger, not less strange. If interstellar comets look like Borisov, what was 'Oumuamua?

In 2021, Avi Loeb published a book: "Extraterrestrial: The First Sign of Intelligent Life Beyond Earth." He remains the only mainstream Harvard professor publicly arguing we may have already been visited.

💭 Final Thought

"The only way to destigmatize the taboo subject of UFOs is to lead by example — by showing that it's fine for a serious scientist to consider this as a hypothesis." - Professor Avi Loeb, Harvard University. A messenger from another star came, accelerated impossibly, and left. We still don't know what it was. We never will.
